       The following are references to year 1987 in fiction:                                                              January 1 &ndash; Frobisher Bay, Northwest Territories, changes its name to Iqaluit. January 2 &ndash; Chadian–Libyan conflict &ndash; Battle of Fada: The Chadian army destroys a Libyan armoured brigade. January 3 &ndash; Aretha Franklin becomes the first woman inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame. January 4 &ndash; 1987 Maryland train collision: An Amtrak train en route from Washington, D.C. to Boston collides with Conrail engines at Chase, Maryland, killing 16. January 5 &ndash; U.S. President Ronald Reagan undergoes prostate surgery, causing speculation about his physical fitness to continue in office. January 8 &ndash; The Dow Jones Industrial Average closes for the first time above 2,000, gaining 8.30 to close at 2,002.25. January 13 &ndash; New York mafiosi Anthony "Fat Tony" Salerno and Carmine Peruccia are sentenced to 100 years in prison for racketeering. January 15 &ndash; Hu Yaobang, General Secretary of the Communist Party of China, is forced into retirement by political conservatives January 16 &ndash; León Febres Cordero, president of Ecuador, is kidnapped by followers of imprisoned general Frank Vargas, who successfully demand the latter's release. January 20 &ndash; Terry Waite, the special envoy of the Archbishop of Canterbury in Lebanon, is kidnapped in Beirut (released November 1991). January 22 &ndash; Pennsylvania Treasurer Budd Dwyer shoots and kills himself with a revolver during a televised press conference after being found guilty on charges of bribery, fraud, conspiracy, and racketeering. January 29 &ndash; William J. Casey ends his term as Director of the Central Intelligence Agency. January 31 &ndash; The last Ohrbach's department store closes in New York City after 64 years of operation. February 11 British Airways is privatised and listed on the London Stock Exchange. The new Constitution of the Philippines goes into effect. This new constitution adds Spanish and Arabic as optional languages of the Philippines. February 20 &ndash; A second Unabomber bomb explodes at a Salt Lake City computer store, injuring the owner. February 23 &ndash; SN 1987A, the first "naked-eye" supernova since 1604, is observed. February 25 Beginning of the Phosphorite War protest movement in Estonian SSR. St. Kitts and Nevis recognizes the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic. February 26 &ndash; Iran–Contra affair: The Tower Commission rebukes U.S. President Ronald Reagan for not controlling his National Security Council staff. February 27 &ndash; Antigua and Barbuda recognizes the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic. March 2 &ndash; American Motors is acquired by the Chrysler Corporation. March 4 &ndash; U.S. President Ronald Reagan addresses the American people on the Iran–Contra affair, acknowledging that his overtures to Iran had 'deteriorated' into an arms-for-hostages deal. March 6 &ndash; Zeebrugge disaster: Roll-on/roll-off cross-channel ferry  capsizes off Zeebrugge harbor in Belgium; 193 people die. March 7 &ndash; 1987 Lieyu massacre: Republic of China Army execute 19 unarmed Vietnamese refugees on Donggang beach, Lieyu, Kinmen off Mainland China. March 9 &ndash; The Irish rock band U2 releases their studio album The Joshua Tree. March 18 &ndash; Woodstock of physics: The marathon session of the American Physical Society’s meeting features 51 presentations concerning the science of high-temperature superconductors. March 20 &ndash; AZT is approved by the United States Food and Drug Administration for use in the treatment of HIV/AIDS. March 24 &ndash; Michael Eisner, CEO of The Walt Disney Company, and French Prime Minister and future President of France, Jacques Chirac, sign the agreement to construct the  Euro Disney Resort (now called Disneyland Paris) and to develop the Val d'Europe area of the new town Marne-la-Vallée in Paris, France. March 29 &ndash; The World Wrestling Federation (later WWE) produces WrestleMania III from the Pontiac Silverdome in Pontiac, Michigan. The event is particularly notable for the record attendance of 93,173, the largest recorded attendance for a live indoor sporting event in North America until February 14, 2010, when the 2010 NBA All-Star Game has an attendance of 108,713 at AT&T Stadium. March 31 &ndash; Margaret Thatcher, Prime Minister of the United Kingdom, is given a 45-minute interview on television in the Soviet Union. April 3 &ndash; Showboat Casino Hotel first opens in Atlantic City, New Jersey. April 13 &ndash; The governments of the Portuguese Republic and the People's Republic of China sign an agreement in which Macau will be returned to China in 1999. April 19 &ndash; The Simpsons cartoon first appears as a series of shorts on The Tracey Ullman Show. April 27 &ndash; The United States Department of Justice declares incumbent Austrian president Kurt Waldheim an "undesirable alien". April 30 &ndash; Canadian Prime Minister Brian Mulroney and the Provincial Premiers agree on principle to the Meech Lake Accord which would bring Quebec into the constitution. May 9 &ndash; A Soviet-made Ilyushin Il-62 airliner, operated by LOT Polish Airlines, crashes into a forest just outside Warsaw, killing all 183 people on board. May 11 &ndash; Klaus Barbie goes on trial in Lyon for war crimes committed during World War II. May 14 &ndash; Lieutenant Colonel Sitiveni Rabuka executes a bloodless coup in Fiji. May 17 &ndash;  is hit by two Iraqi-owned Exocet AM39 air-to-surface missiles killing 47 sailors. May 22 The Hashimpura massacre occurs in Meerut, India. The first ever Rugby World Cup kicks off with New Zealand playing Italy at Eden Park, Auckland. May 27 &ndash; In one of the densest concentrations of humanity in history, a crowd of 800,000+ packed shoulder-to-shoulder onto the Golden Gate Bridge and its approaches for its 50th Anniversary celebration. May 28 &ndash; Eighteen-year-old West German pilot Mathias Rust evades Soviet air defenses and lands a private plane on Red Square in Moscow. He is immediately detained (released on August 3, 1988). June 3 &ndash; The Vanuatu Labour Party is founded. June 8 &ndash; The New Zealand Nuclear Free Zone, Disarmament, and Arms Control Act is passed, the first of its kind in the world. June 11 &ndash; The Conservative Party of the United Kingdom, led by Margaret Thatcher, is re-elected for a third term at the 1987 general election. June 12 &ndash; During a visit to Berlin, Germany, U.S. President Ronald Reagan challenges Soviet Premier Mikhail Gorbachev to tear down the Berlin Wall. June 15 &ndash; Rock musician Roger Waters releases his first solo album since splitting from his former band, Pink Floyd, entitled Radio K.A.O.S. June 17 &ndash; With the death of the last individual, the dusky seaside sparrow becomes extinct. June 19 Teddy Seymour is officially designated the first black man to sail around the world, when he completes his solo sailing circumnavigation in Frederiksted, St. Croix, of the United States Virgin Islands. Edwards v. Aguillard: The Supreme Court of the United States rules that a Louisiana law requiring that creation science be taught in public schools whenever evolution is taught is unconstitutional. June 27 &ndash; A commercial HS 748 (Philippine Airlines Flight 206) crashes near Baguio City, Philippines, killing 50. June 28 Iraqi warplanes drop mustard-gas bombs on the Iranian town of Sardasht in two separate bombing rounds, on four residential areas. This is the first time a civilian town was targeted by chemical weapons. An accidental explosion at the Hohenfels Training Area in West Germany kills 3 U.S. troops. June 29 &ndash; South Korean president Roh Tae-woo makes a speech promising a wide program of nationwide reforms, the result of the June Democracy Movement. June 30 &ndash; Canada introduces a one-dollar coin, nicknamed the "Loonie". July 1 The Single European Act is passed by the European Community. U.S. President Ronald Reagan nominates former Solicitor General Robert Bork to the Supreme Court. The nomination is later rejected by the Senate. July 3 In the Soviet Union, Vladimir Nikolayev is sentenced to death for cannibalism. Greater Manchester Police recover the body of 16-year-old Pauline Reade from Saddleworth Moor, after her killers Ian Brady and Myra Hindley help them in their search, almost exactly 24 years since Pauline was last seen alive. July 4 &ndash; A court in Lyon sentences former Gestapo boss Klaus Barbie to life imprisonment for crimes against humanity. July 11 Australian Prime Minister Robert Hawke's government is re-elected for a third term. World population is estimated to have reached five billion people, according to the United Nations. July 12 &ndash; Konami releases Metal Gear (video game) in Japan for the MSX2. July 17 &ndash; The Dow Jones Industrial Average closes above the 2,500 mark for the first time, at 2,510.04. July 22 &ndash; Palestinian cartoonist Naji Salim al-Ali is shot in London; he dies August 28. Docklands Light Railway in London, the first driverless railway in Great Britain, is formally opened by Elizabeth II. The Federal Communications Commission rescinds the Fairness Doctrine, which had required radio and television stations to "fairly" present controversial issues. August 7 &ndash; The Colombian frigate Caldas enters Venezuelan waters near the Los Monjes Archipelago, sparking the Caldas frigate crisis between both nations. August 9 &ndash; Hoddle Street massacre in Australia: Julian Knight, 19, goes on a shooting rampage in the Melbourne suburb of Clifton Hill, Victoria, killing 7 people and injuring 19 before surrendering to police. August 14 &ndash; All the children held at Kai Lama, a rural property on Lake Eildon, Australia, run by the Santiniketan Park Association, are released after a police raid. August 16 Northwest Airlines Flight 255 (a McDonnell Douglas MD-82) crashes on takeoff from Detroit Metropolitan Airport in Romulus, Michigan just west of Detroit killing all but one (4-year old Cecelia Cichan) of the 156 people on board. The followers of the Harmonic Convergence claim it was observed around the world. August 17 &ndash; Rudolf Hess is found dead in his cell in Spandau Prison. Hess, 93, is believed to have committed suicide by hanging himself with an electrical flex. He was the last remaining prisoner at the complex, which is soon demolished. August 19 Hungerford massacre: Sixteen people die in the first British mass shooting carried out by Michael Ryan.
